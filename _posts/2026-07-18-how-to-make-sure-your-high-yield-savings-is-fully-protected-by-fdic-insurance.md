> **The short version:** High-yield savings accounts (HYSAs), CDs, and money market accounts are covered by [FDIC](https://www.fdic.gov/resources/deposit-insurance) insurance up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. To ensure full protection, check your account limits and consider spreading your funds across multiple banks.

To safeguard your funds in high-yield savings accounts, it's essential to understand how FDIC insurance works. Many individuals are unaware that various ownership categories can significantly impact their coverage. This piece will clarify how you can make certain that your high-yield savings, certificates of deposit (CDs), and money market accounts enjoy maximum protection under FDIC insurance.

## What Is FDIC Insurance?

FDIC, or the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, is an independent agency of the U.S. government that protects depositors by providing insurance for deposits held at insured banks. If a bank fails, the FDIC ensures that depositors are compensated up to the limit set for their accounts.

### How Much Coverage Does the FDIC Provide?

The standard coverage limit for FDIC insurance is $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, for each ownership category. This means that if you have accounts at multiple banks, you can safely have more than $250,000 covered. For example, if you have $250,000 at Bank A and another $250,000 at Bank B, both amounts are fully insured.

## How to Ensure Your High-Yield Savings Is Fully Protected

To ensure complete protection of your savings, follow these critical steps:

1. **Know Your Bank's Status**: Confirm that your bank is FDIC-insured. You can use the [FDIC's BankFind Suite](https://banks.data.fdic.gov/bankfind-suite/bankfind) to check an institution’s insurance status.
2. **Understand Ownership Categories**: Deposits in different ownership categories (individual, joint, retirement accounts) are insured separately. For example, an individual account and a joint account you share with someone else would each have their own separate $250,000 coverage.
3. **Spread Out Your Accounts**: If your total deposits exceed $250,000 at a single bank, consider distributing your funds across several banks to ensure each portion remains within the insured limit.
4. **Review Withdrawals and Access**: Some banks might impose withdrawal limits on high-yield savings accounts. While the federal regulation restricting withdrawals was suspended in 2020, it's wise to check each institution's specific rules, as they may still set their own limits.

### What Are the Different Types of Ownership Categories?

Understanding the various ownership categories can help you maximize your FDIC coverage:

- **Individual Accounts**: Owned by one person. Coverage is up to $250,000.
- **Joint Accounts**: Owned by two or more people. Coverage is up to $250,000 per co-owner.
- **Retirement Accounts**: Includes IRAs, which have federal insurance separate from other accounts, covering up to $250,000.
- **Trust Accounts**: These can vary in coverage based on the specifics of the trust.

Seeing how these categories work is vital. You can protect substantially more than $250,000 by properly structuring your accounts.

This overview highlights that all three account types are covered by FDIC insurance, but differ in terms of interest rates and accessibility.

## Can You Have Multiple Accounts at One Bank?

Yes, you can have multiple accounts at one bank, but coverage still depends on the ownership category. For instance, if you have a personal high-yield savings account and a joint account with a spouse at the same bank, your individual account will be insured for $250,000, as will the joint account. This effectively doubles your insured deposits at that institution.

### What If You Exceed the Limit?

If your deposits exceed the $250,000 limit at a single bank, consider taking these actions:

- **Open Accounts at Different Banks**: This is the most straightforward way to ensure full FDIC coverage. 
- **Opt for Multiple Ownership Categories**: If you want to keep your money in a single institution, consider having accounts with varied ownership types. 

Be aware that the thresholds can pile up quite quickly, especially when you add money market accounts and CDs into the mix, which may also necessitate distribution into several institutions for maximum protection.
