> **Quick answer:** FDIC insurance protects your savings accounts, including high-yield savings accounts, CDs, and money market accounts, up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category. This safeguard ensures that even if the bank fails, your deposits are protected within the coverage limits.

Mistakenly believing money is at risk in standard savings accounts can deter people from opening these accounts, despite the security FDIC insurance offers. Understanding how this insurance works can help you make informed decisions about protecting your savings.

## What is FDIC Insurance and How Does It Work?

FDIC insurance is a safeguard provided by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation, an independent agency of the U.S. government. It protects depositors by insuring deposits in member banks up to the insured limit of $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. This means if your bank fails, you will receive your insured deposits back, typically within a few weeks.

The FDIC was created during the Great Depression to restore public confidence in the banking system. Today, it covers various types of deposit accounts, including high-yield savings accounts, certificates of deposit (CDs), and money market accounts.

## What Accounts Are Covered by FDIC Insurance?

FDIC insurance covers several types of accounts. Here’s a breakdown:

- **High-Yield Savings Accounts**: These accounts typically offer higher interest rates than traditional savings accounts and are fully insured by the FDIC, so your balance is protected within the coverage limits.

- **Certificates of Deposit (CDs)**: When you purchase a CD, your money is locked in for a specific term, but like savings accounts, they are also covered by FDIC insurance.

- **Money Market Accounts**: These accounts offer higher interest rates and come with check-writing privileges. They are insured like savings accounts, providing similar protections.

## How Much Coverage Does FDIC Insurance Provide?

Understanding the coverage limits is crucial. The standard limit is $250,000 per depositor, for each insured bank and for each ownership category. This means you could have more than $250,000 insured at the same bank if the funds are held in different ownership categories, such as personal accounts, joint accounts, and retirement accounts.

For example, if you have a personal savings account and a joint account with your spouse at the same bank, both accounts would provide up to $250,000 coverage separately, leading to a total of $500,000 in coverage.

## How Is FDIC Insurance Different from NCUA Insurance?

While the FDIC covers banks, credit unions are covered by the National Credit Union Administration (NCUA). Similar to the FDIC, NCUA also provides insurance up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured credit union, per ownership category. This assurance allows consumers to feel safe regardless of whether they choose a bank or a credit union.

It's essential to check whether your financial institution is insured. You can use the FDIC's BankFind Suite to confirm a bank is insured or learn if your credit union has NCUA insurance.

## How Should You Choose an Insured Financial Institution?

When selecting a bank or credit union, consider the following factors:

1. **Insurance Coverage**: Ensure that the institution is federally insured by the FDIC or NCUA.
   
2. **Interest Rates**: Compare the APYs offered by different institutions, especially for high-yield savings accounts and CDs, as these can vary significantly.

3. **Fees**: Look out for any maintenance fees or withdrawal penalties, especially with high-yield accounts or CDs, to avoid unnecessary charges.

4. **Account Features**: Evaluate features like ATM access, online banking capabilities, and accessibility to ensure they meet your needs.

5. **Customer Service**: Consider reviews and ratings of the institution's customer service to ensure support is readily available when needed.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thinking All Accounts Are Insured**: Many people do not realize that not all deposit accounts come with insurance.   
   - **Why it matters**: Without insurance, deposits are at risk if a bank fails. Always verify insurance status.

2. **Confusing FDIC with NCUA**: Some savers assume that all financial institutions are covered by FDIC.  
   - **Why it matters**: Understanding which agency covers your funds can ensure maximum safety depending on whether you use a bank or credit union.

3. **Ignoring Ownership Categories**: Many depositors overlook how account structures affect insurance limits.  
   - **Why it matters**: Proper structuring can protect more of your savings beyond the standard $250,000 limit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What happens if my bank fails?

If your bank fails, the FDIC will step in to cover your insured deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank. It’s arranged so that depositors typically receive their funds within a few weeks.

### Are online banks FDIC insured?

Most legitimate online banks are FDIC-insured. The FDIC's BankFind Suite can help you verify if your online bank holds the necessary insurance.

### Can I have multiple accounts and still be insured?

Yes, you can have multiple accounts at the same bank, and as long as they are in different ownership categories, each account can be insured up to the standard limit.

### What types of accounts are not covered by FDIC insurance?

FDIC insurance does not cover securities, mutual funds, or life insurance policies. It's important to check the specifics of what is covered before making an investment.

### How can I ensure I’m within the insurance limits?

Keep track of your total deposits across all accounts at any given bank and consider separating ownership categories or using multiple banks if your deposits exceed $250,000.

## What to Do If Your Bank Fails

In the unfortunate event that your bank fails, it's essential to understand the steps that follow to secure your insured deposits. While the FDIC steps in to protect you, here’s [what you should](/what-you-should-know-about-early-withdrawal-penalties-on-cds/) expect and do:

1. **Stay Informed**: If you hear news about your bank facing financial difficulty, monitor the situation closely. The FDIC usually informs customers via mail if a bank has been closed, so keep an eye on your postal mail and online banking notifications.

2. **FDIC Response**: When a bank fails, the FDIC acts swiftly to take control of the assets. They will either sell the bank to another financial institution or pay out deposits directly to customers. Usually, the goal is to ensure access to your funds as quickly as possible.

3. **Accessing Your Funds**: You will typically have immediate access to your accounts within a few days post-failure, either through the acquiring bank (if one exists) or through a direct payout from the FDIC. If the bank is sold, you’ll likely receive a new account number or procedures to follow for withdrawal.

4. **Claim Procedure for Larger Accounts**: If your deposits exceed the FDIC insurance limit of $250,000, you will receive payouts for the insured amounts first, and for amounts over the limit, you may need to file a claim. The FDIC provides guidance on filing claims to recover uninsured deposits.

5. **Record Keeping**: Keeping thorough records of your account statements can help ensure the claims process is smooth. If you face challenges verifying your balance or making a claim, having documentation will support your case.

By understanding these steps, you can alleviate some anxiety around bank failures, knowing your funds are protected, and that there is a well-defined process for accessing your insured money.
