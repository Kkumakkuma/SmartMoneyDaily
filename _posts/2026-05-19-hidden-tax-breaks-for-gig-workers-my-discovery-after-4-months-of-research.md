## What Tax Breaks Are Available for Gig Workers? 

### 1. Home Office Deduction

**How does the home office deduction work?**  
If you use a portion of your home strictly for business purposes, you may qualify for a home office deduction. This can include a percentage of rent or mortgage interest, utilities, and Internet costs. 

The home office deduction provides two methods for calculation:

- **Simplified Method:** This allows a deduction of $5 per square foot of home office space. For example, if your office is 200 square feet, you’d claim $1,000.
- **Regular Method:** Involves itemizing actual expenses related to your business use. Suppose your home office occupies 15% of your home (1,500 square feet); if your total home-related expenses for the year are $15,000, you can deduct $2,250 (15% of $15,000).

### 2. Mileage Deduction

**Is the mileage deduction worth it in 2026?**  
Yes! For the 2026 tax year, you can deduct $0.65 per mile driven for business purposes. If you’re a rideshare driver or make deliveries, you could end up saving significant amounts based on how far you drive. 

Here’s how I utilized the mileage deduction last year: I drove approximately 5,000 business-related miles in 2023, which means I was eligible for a $3,250 deduction. Keep in mind that it’s essential to maintain proper documentation, such as a mileage log or use an app designed to track mileage effortlessly.

### 3. Health Insurance Premiums

**How can I deduct health insurance premiums as a gig worker?**  
As a self-employed individual, you can deduct health insurance premiums, which is a substantial relief for many gig workers. This deduction applies not just to your premiums but also for your spouse and dependents. 

Last year, I spent $4,800 on health insurance for myself and covered my family, reducing my taxable income significantly. It’s worth exploring whether your premiums qualify, especially if you purchase insurance independently. According to IRS Publication 535, if you were eligible to enroll in an employer's plan but opted for your own, you may still deduct those costs.

### 4. Retirement Contributions

**How can I maximize retirement contributions to minimize tax liability?**  
Contributing to a retirement account not only secures your future but can also provide significant deductions. Specifically, if you establish a solo 401(k) or a SEP IRA, you can deduct contributions from your taxable income.

As an example, I contributed $18,000 to my solo 401(k) in 2023, which reduced my taxable income substantially. According to the IRS, the contributions could be a maximum of 25% of your net earnings from self-employment or up to $61,000 in total contributions for individuals under 50 in 2026, depending on the account type. 

### 5. Equipment and Supplies

**What kind of equipment can I deduct as a gig worker?**  
As a gig worker, you can deduct the cost of tools and equipment necessary for your work, such as computers, cameras, or software. This was a game changer when I started freelancing—it allows you to recover a portion of what you invest in your business infrastructure.

For example, I purchased a laptop for $1,200 and editing software for $600, making my total business-related equipment deductible. I reported these expenses in the relevant tax year and filed them accurately, maximizing my tax returns. Keep in mind that items over a certain dollar amount may need to be depreciated over time rather than written off all at once.

### 6. Educational Expenses

**What are the rules for deducting educational expenses?**  
If you take courses to improve your skills or knowledge for your gig work, you can qualify for tax deductions. This includes online courses, webinars, or even conferences that enhance your ability to perform your job. 

I took a $1,200 course on digital marketing this past fall, which directly improved my income-earning potentials within my freelance business. According to IRS guidelines, you need to ensure the training is directly related to your current gig.
